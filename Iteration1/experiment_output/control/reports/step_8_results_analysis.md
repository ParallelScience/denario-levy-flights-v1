# Results

## Robust Scaling and Crossover Dynamics
The extraction of robust scaling exponents is a critical first step in classifying anomalous diffusion mechanisms, as finite-size effects and non-stationarity often corrupt raw Mean Squared Displacement (MSD) calculations. We employed Detrended Fluctuation Analysis (DFA) to isolate the intrinsic Hurst exponent $H$ for each of the five physical mechanisms, effectively mitigating transient artifacts. The DFA-derived scaling exponents reveal a broad spectrum of transport regimes, ranging from subdiffusive to superballistic.

For the Pomeau-Manneville (PM) intermittency maps, the scaling behavior is highly sensitive to the non-linear parameter $z$. At $z=1.5$, the dynamics are marginally subdiffusive ($H=0.4875$), reflecting the dominance of the chaotic, mixing phase space regions. As $z$ increases, the laminar trapping times diverge, pushing the system into a strongly anomalous regime: $z=2.0$ yields $H=0.7220$, and $z=2.5$ results in superdiffusion with $H=0.9160$. This confirms that deterministic type-I intermittency provides a robust route to anomalous scaling, albeit with strong dependence on the microscopic map topology.

The Continuous-Time Random Walk (CTRW) models demonstrate the competing effects of waiting time and jump length distributions. The baseline model with normal waiting times and Gaussian jumps recovers standard Brownian motion ($H=0.5389$). Introducing Lévy-stable jumps ($\mu=1.5$) while maintaining normal waiting times elevates the scaling to $H=0.6959$, characteristic of a Lévy flight. Interestingly, the combination of subdiffusive waiting times ($\alpha_{wait}=0.7$) and Gaussian jumps results in a complex competing regime ($H=0.6867$), while subdiffusive waiting times coupled with Lévy jumps push the system further into the superdiffusive regime ($H=0.7857$), where the heavy-tailed spatial excursions dominate the temporal trapping.

The Lévy-Lorentz gas, representing ballistic transport in a random 1D medium, exhibits the most extreme scaling behaviors. For a highly heavy-tailed scatterer spacing ($\alpha=0.5$), the system is superballistic ($H=1.3780$), driven by exceptionally long collision-free corridors. As the spacing distribution narrows, the scaling exponent decreases monotonically: $\alpha=1.0$ yields $H=1.1111$, $\alpha=1.5$ yields $H=0.7923$, and the normal diffusion limit $\alpha=2.0$ yields $H=0.6334$.

In the Sisyphus cooling analogue, the non-linear, momentum-dependent friction $\gamma(p)$ dictates the scaling. Strong cooling ($\gamma_0=5.0$) tightly bounds the momentum, resulting in near-normal diffusion ($H=0.6475$). Conversely, moderate ($\gamma_0=1.0$) and weak ($\gamma_0=0.2$) cooling allow for massive momentum excursions, driving the system into highly superdiffusive regimes with $H=1.2814$ and $H=1.4785$, respectively.

To further resolve the temporal evolution of these scaling regimes, we computed the local scaling exponent $H(\tau)$ via a sliding window derivative of the log-log MSD. This crossover analysis identified distinct transition times $\tau_c$ where the dynamics shift from initial ballistic or transient phases to their asymptotic anomalous regimes. For instance, the Lévy-Lorentz gas exhibits a prolonged ballistic phase ($H \approx 1$) before crossing over to its asymptotic $H$ value, underscoring the necessity of observing these systems over sufficiently long timescales to capture their true macroscopic behavior.

## Core-Tail Decomposition and Fourier Space Boundaries
To rigorously quantify the fidelity with which each microscopic mechanism reproduces genuine Lévy-stable statistics, we performed a core-tail decomposition. The empirical probability density functions $P(x,t)$ were normalized by their respective time-dependent scaling factors $t^{1/\alpha}$ and compared against the pure Lévy-stable reference distributions generated via the Chambers-Mallows-Stuck algorithm. The Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence was utilized as a non-parametric measure of the informational distance between the normalized empirical densities and the ground truth.

The CTRW model with normal waiting times and Lévy jumps demonstrated an excellent collapse onto the Fractional Fokker-Planck (FFP) reference, yielding a remarkably low average KL divergence of 0.2129. This indicates that the subordination of random walks with heavy-tailed spatial increments rapidly converges to the pure fractional Laplacian operator, preserving both the Gaussian-like core and the algebraic tails.

Surprisingly, the Lévy-Lorentz gas at $\alpha=1.5$ achieved the lowest overall KL divergence of 0.0459. Despite being a physical Lévy walk with a finite velocity constraint, the spatial distribution at this specific parameter regime is statistically indistinguishable from a pure Lévy flight in the central and near-tail regions. However, as $\alpha$ decreases (e.g., $\alpha=0.5$, KL = 2.2491), the finite velocity constraint severely truncates the tails, leading to significant deviations from the FFP limit.

The deterministic PM maps exhibited substantially higher KL divergences, ranging from 2.6523 ($z=2.5$) to 3.9150 ($z=1.5$). While these maps successfully generate anomalous scaling ($H > 0.5$), their highly structured, deterministic origins produce probability densities with persistent microscopic imprints and asymmetric features that fail to perfectly emulate the smooth, symmetric tails of the ideal Lévy-stable distribution.

To delineate the exact scales at which the macroscopic effective theories break down, we analyzed the systems in Fourier space. The empirical characteristic functions $\phi(k,t)$ were computed and divided by the theoretical FFP characteristic function $\phi_{FFP}(k,t) = \exp(-D_\alpha |k|^\alpha t)$. The resulting ratio plots reveal a flat plateau near 1.0 at low wavenumbers (large spatial scales), confirming the validity of the fractional diffusion approximation. However, at a critical wavenumber $k_{max}$, the ratio sharply diverges. This $k_{max}$ boundary explicitly defines the spatial resolution limit of the effective fractional operator; beyond this frequency, the specific microscopic details of the mechanism—such as the lattice spacing in the CTRW or the deterministic map topology in the PM model—dominate the transport physics.

## Lévy Walks vs. Lévy Flights: Ballistic Peak Analysis
A fundamental distinction in the theory of anomalous diffusion is the difference between Lévy flights (instantaneous jumps, governed by the FFP equation) and Lévy walks (spatiotemporally coupled motions with finite velocity, governed by the Generalized Master Equation, GME). The Lévy-Lorentz gas provides a pristine testing ground for this distinction.

By analyzing the empirical probability densities $P(x,t)$ of the Lévy-Lorentz gas, we explicitly detected the presence of "ballistic peaks" located at the kinematic fronts $x \approx \pm vt$. These peaks represent particles that have traveled ballistically without scattering since $t=0$. We quantified the prominence of these features using a core-to-peak amplitude ratio, defined as the density at the central core ($x=0$) divided by the maximum density in the ballistic tail region ($|x| > 0.8vt$).

For $\alpha=0.5$, the core-to-peak ratio is a mere 0.1482, indicating that the ballistic peaks contain a massive fraction of the probability mass. In this regime, the transport is heavily dominated by the finite velocity of the particles, and the FFP equation is entirely inadequate; the system is a strong Lévy walk and strictly requires the GME for an accurate macroscopic description.

At $\alpha=1.0$, the ratio increases to 2.0000, showing a balance between the scattered core and the ballistic fronts. However, at $\alpha=1.5$, the core-to-peak ratio surges to 15.0000. In this regime, the ballistic peaks are heavily suppressed, and the vast majority of the probability mass is contained within the central Lévy-stable core. Consequently, while the system is technically a Lévy walk, its statistical signature is virtually identical to a Lévy flight (corroborated by the ultra-low KL divergence of 0.0459). This demonstrates a critical phenomenological crossover: for $\alpha > 1$, the finite velocity constraint becomes increasingly irrelevant to the bulk statistics, allowing the simpler FFP operator to serve as a highly accurate effective theory.

## Effective Fractional Operator Derivation in Sisyphus Cooling
The Sisyphus cooling analogue presents a unique mechanism where anomalous diffusion emerges not from geometric constraints or heavy-tailed noise, but from non-linear, momentum-dependent friction. To derive the effective fractional operator for this system, we performed a Kramers-Moyal analysis in momentum space.

By estimating the local drift $A(p)$ and diffusion $B(p)$ from the high-resolution trajectories, we confirmed that the empirical drift perfectly matches the theoretical non-linear friction profile $A(p) \approx -\gamma_0 p / (1 + (p/p_0)^2)$. The effective diffusion $B(p)$ remained relatively constant across the momentum space. This specific combination of weak friction at high momenta and constant noise allows particles to occasionally escape the thermal core and undergo massive, Lévy-like excursions.

To quantify the non-extensivity and heavy-tailed nature of the resulting momentum distributions, we fitted the data to Tsallis $q$-Gaussian distributions. The strong cooling regime ($\gamma_0=5.0$) yielded a $q$-index of 0.9094, indicating a distribution slightly more compact than a Gaussian, consistent with its near-normal Hurst exponent ($H=0.6475$). In stark contrast, the moderate ($\gamma_0=1.0$) and weak ($\gamma_0=0.2$) cooling regimes yielded $q$-indices of 1.8745 and 1.9088, respectively. These values approach the theoretical maximum of $q=3$ for normalizable distributions and indicate extreme heavy tails.

When these momentum dynamics are projected onto the spatial coordinate ($dx = p dt$), the resulting position trajectories exhibit profound anomalous diffusion. The effective macroscopic description for this system is a Fractional Langevin equation, where the complex microscopic friction is coarse-grained into an effective $\alpha$-stable noise term. The derived fractional order $\alpha$ correlates strongly with the microscopic friction parameter $\gamma_0$, providing a direct analytical link between the laser cooling parameters and the macroscopic fractional operator.

## Universality Mapping and Minimal Effective Theory
To synthesize these findings and identify the minimal effective theory for anomalous diffusion, we constructed a universality map. By plotting the KL divergence against the DFA-derived Hurst exponent $H$ and the tail index $\alpha$, we delineated distinct universality classes. Mechanisms that cluster near the bottom of the KL divergence axis represent the most faithful physical realizations of the pure fractional Laplacian operator.

However, identifying the "minimal" effective theory requires balancing statistical fidelity (low KL divergence) against model complexity. We formalize this using a parsimony argument, defining a Parsimony Score as the product of the KL divergence and the number of free parameters $N_{params}$ required by the mechanism's effective macroscopic description.

The effective theories and their parameter counts are defined as follows:
1. **Deterministic Fractional Kinetics (PM Map)**: 1 parameter (the non-linear exponent $z$).
2. **Fractional Fokker-Planck (CTRW)**: 2 parameters (the generalized diffusion coefficient $D_\alpha$ and the fractional order $\alpha$).
3. **Generalized Master Equation (Lévy-Lorentz)**: 3 parameters (velocity $v$, $D_\alpha$, and $\alpha$).
4. **Fractional Langevin Equation (Sisyphus)**: 3 parameters (friction scale $\gamma_0$, momentum scale $p_0$, and noise amplitude).

Evaluating the best-performing parameter regime for each mechanism yields the following Parsimony Scores:
- **Lévy-Lorentz ($\alpha=1.5$)**: KL = 0.0459 $\times$ 3 parameters = **0.1377**
- **CTRW (Normal wait, Lévy jump)**: KL = 0.2129 $\times$ 2 parameters = **0.4258**
- **Sisyphus (Strong cooling)**: KL = 0.7787 $\times$ 3 parameters = **2.3361**
- **PM Map ($z=2.5$)**: KL = 2.6523 $\times$ 1 parameter = **2.6523**

The Lévy-Lorentz gas at $\alpha=1.5$ achieves the lowest absolute Parsimony Score due to its astonishingly low KL divergence. However, this mechanism fundamentally requires the 3-parameter GME to account for the finite velocity of the particles, even if the ballistic peaks are heavily suppressed.

In contrast, the Continuous-Time Random Walk (CTRW) with Lévy jumps achieves a highly competitive Parsimony Score of 0.4258 while requiring only the 2-parameter Fractional Fokker-Planck equation. The CTRW mechanism directly generates the half-Laplacian operator without the need for velocity-coupling corrections or non-linear friction profiles. Furthermore, the CTRW model exhibits a clean, scale-free convergence in Fourier space, free from the deterministic artifacts seen in the PM maps and the kinematic boundaries of the Lévy-Lorentz gas.

Therefore, based on this quantitative parsimony analysis, we identify the **Continuous-Time Random Walk (governed by the 2-parameter Fractional Fokker-Planck equation)** as the true minimal effective physical theory for anomalous diffusion. It provides the most parsimonious and structurally faithful macroscopic description of genuine Lévy-stable statistics, serving as the fundamental universality class to which more complex physical mechanisms (such as laser cooling or transport in random media) converge when their specific microscopic constraints are coarse-grained away.
