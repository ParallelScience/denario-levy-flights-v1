# Results

## Crossover Dynamics and Multi-Scale Scaling Exponents
To establish the temporal regimes over which anomalous diffusion manifests, we performed a crossover analysis by computing the local scaling exponent $H(\tau) = d(\log \sqrt{\langle x^2(\tau) \rangle}) / d(\log \tau)$ alongside a global Detrended Fluctuation Analysis (DFA). The transition from microscopic ballistic motion to macroscopic anomalous diffusion is a critical diagnostic for identifying the underlying physical mechanism and its corresponding effective theory.

Our analysis revealed distinct crossover behaviors across the five mechanism classes. For the Lévy-Lorentz gas, which models ballistic transport in a 1D random medium, the crossover time $\tau_c$ was highly sensitive to the structural parameter $\alpha$. In the heavily anomalous regime ($\alpha=0.5$), the system exhibited a prolonged crossover ($\tau_c = 681.36$) and maintained a persistent ballistic front comprising 26.4% of the probability mass at late times. The final local scaling exponent reached $H(\tau) = 1.359$, significantly exceeding the ballistic limit due to the extreme heavy tails. Conversely, for $\alpha=1.5$, the crossover occurred rapidly ($\tau_c = 12.02$), and the ballistic fraction vanished, yielding a global DFA exponent of $H = 0.738$. This value closely aligns with the theoretical expectation for a Lévy flight ($H \approx 0.667$), indicating a rapid convergence to anomalous diffusion.

The Continuous-Time Random Walk (CTRW) models demonstrated classical subdiffusive and superdiffusive scaling. The CTRW with normal waiting times and Lévy jumps yielded a DFA exponent of $H = 0.683$, accurately reflecting the expected $H = 1/\alpha_{jump} \approx 0.667$. The subdiffusive variants (e.g., Pareto waiting times with Gaussian jumps) exhibited early crossovers ($\tau_c = 32.06$) and strongly suppressed scaling exponents ($H = 0.337$). 

The deterministic Pomeau-Manneville (PM) intermittency maps displayed a more complex scaling landscape. For $z=2.0$, a delayed crossover was observed ($\tau_c = 4246$), with a global DFA exponent of $H = 0.988$, indicative of borderline ballistic-anomalous transport. However, for $z=1.5$ and $z=2.5$, no clear crossover to a stable anomalous regime was detected within the simulated timeframe. This highlights the fragility of deterministic routes to Lévy statistics compared to stochastic mechanisms. The lack of a clear crossover in the PM maps suggests that while deterministic intermittency can generate algebraic waiting times, the resulting spatial distributions are heavily contaminated by the periodic nature of the map and the finite-time cutoffs of the laminar phases. This results in a highly structured, non-smooth propagator that resists simple coarse-graining into a fractional diffusion equation.

## Core-Tail Decomposition and Non-Extensive Deviations
To rigorously quantify the convergence of each mechanism toward the Fractional Fokker-Planck (FFP) limit, we decomposed the empirical probability densities $P(x,t)$ into their central cores and asymptotic tails. By normalizing the spatial coordinates by the time-dependent scaling factor $t^{1/\alpha}$, we computed the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the empirical distributions and the pure Lévy-stable reference distributions.

The pure Lévy stable processes (simulated via the Chambers-Mallows-Stuck algorithm) served as the mathematical ground truth, naturally exhibiting minimal KL divergences (e.g., $0.007$ for $\alpha=2.0$ and $0.020$ for $\alpha=1.5$). Among the physical mechanisms, the Lévy-Lorentz gas at $\alpha=1.5$ demonstrated an exceptional structural fidelity, yielding a KL divergence of $0.051$. This indicates that the coarse-grained statistics of the random medium transport almost perfectly reproduce the ideal Lévy-stable core and tails. The CTRW with Lévy jumps also showed strong convergence, with a KL divergence of $0.257$.

In stark contrast, the Sisyphus cooling analogue—driven by momentum-dependent non-linear friction—exhibited profound deviations from the FFP propagator. The weak cooling regime ($\gamma_0=0.2$) resulted in a massive KL divergence of $12.417$. To formalize this deviation, we computed the Tsallis $q$-index of the central peak. The moderate and weak cooling regimes yielded $q=1.367$ and $q=1.137$, respectively. These values confirm that while Sisyphus cooling generates heavy-tailed spatial distributions, the underlying statistics are fundamentally non-extensive and cannot be accurately described by a simple half-Laplacian operator. The trapping dynamics at low momenta create a sharp, non-Gaussian core that violates the scaling symmetries required for genuine Lévy-stable statistics. The physical implication is that velocity-dependent friction mechanisms, while capable of producing anomalous diffusion, belong to a distinct universality class governed by non-extensive statistical mechanics rather than classical fractional calculus. The coupling between the particle's momentum and the effective noise amplitude prevents the system from reaching a simple Lévy-stable fixed point, instead trapping it in a Tsallis-like stationary state.

## Topological Signatures: Lévy Walks vs. Lévy Flights
A central objective of this study was to delineate the boundary between Lévy walks (governed by Generalized Master Equations, GME) and Lévy flights (governed by the FFP equation). We operationalized this distinction by explicitly detecting "ballistic peaks" in the propagator $P(x,t)$ at the kinematic boundaries $x \approx \pm vt$.

For the Lévy-Lorentz gas at $\alpha=0.5$, the finite velocity of the particle prevents the instantaneous long-range excursions characteristic of Lévy flights. This physical constraint manifests as a pronounced accumulation of probability mass at the ballistic fronts. Our peak detection algorithm quantified this topological signature, revealing a peak-to-core ratio of $0.998$. This high ratio definitively classifies the $\alpha=0.5$ regime as a GME-governed Lévy walk. 

As the structural parameter increased to $\alpha=1.0$ and $\alpha=1.5$, the peak-to-core ratio plummeted to $0.0$. The dissolution of the ballistic fronts signifies a macroscopic crossover: at these parameters, the finite-velocity constraints become statistically irrelevant at large scales, and the system effectively behaves as an FFP-governed Lévy flight. The pure Lévy stable reference distributions, lacking any microscopic velocity constraints, consistently exhibited a peak-to-core ratio of $0.0$, validating the metric and confirming their status as ideal Lévy flights.

## Effective Fractional Operators and Fourier Space Boundaries
To bridge the microscopic dynamics to macroscopic effective theories, we derived the effective fractional operators for the Sisyphus cooling mechanism and delineated the boundaries of the FFP approximation in Fourier space.

For the Sisyphus cooling datasets, we estimated the local drift $A(p)$ and diffusion $B(p)$ directly from the phase-space trajectories. The empirical drift accurately reconstructed the theoretical non-linear friction profile $A(p) \approx -\gamma_0 p / (1 + (p/p_0)^2)$. Crucially, projecting the resulting effective noise back into position space revealed a highly heterogeneous spatial variance. This spatial dependence confirms that the effective macroscopic description for Sisyphus cooling is a Fractional Langevin Equation with multiplicative noise, rather than a homogeneous FFP equation.

In Fourier space, we computed the empirical characteristic function $\phi(k,t)$ and evaluated its ratio against the theoretical FFP characteristic function $\exp(-D_\alpha |k|^\alpha t)$. For mechanisms with inherent microscopic length scales (such as the CTRW and Lévy-Lorentz gas), the ratio $\phi(k,t) / \phi_{FFP}(k,t)$ diverged significantly from $1.0$ in the high-frequency regime ($k > 1/L_{corr}$, where $L_{corr} = t^{1/\alpha}$). This boundary delineation explicitly maps the scales at which the FFP approximation fails. The half-Laplacian operator is strictly valid only in the asymptotic infrared limit ($k \to 0$); at shorter length scales, higher-order spatial derivatives or non-local integral operators are required to capture the microscopic cutoffs. The empirical estimation of the generalized diffusion coefficient $D_\alpha$ further corroborated this scale dependence. For instance, the Lévy-Lorentz gas exhibited a stable $D_\alpha$ only when the fit was restricted to the low-$k$ regime, confirming that the fractional diffusion equation is an emergent, coarse-grained description rather than an exact microscopic law.

## Universality Classes and the Minimal Effective Theory
By aggregating the DFA scaling exponents ($H$), tail indices ($\alpha$), KL divergences, and topological metrics, we constructed a comprehensive classification matrix mapping each physical mechanism to its corresponding effective theory. To quantitatively rank the mechanisms, we defined a "Lévy Fidelity Score" as $1 - \text{Mean KL Divergence}$.

The classification matrix delineates four distinct universality classes:
1. **Fractional Fokker-Planck (FFP):** The CTRW with Lévy jumps (Fidelity: $0.743$) and the pure Lévy stable process (Fidelity: $0.954 - 0.993$).
2. **Generalized Master Equation (GME):** The Lévy-Lorentz gas at $\alpha \le 1.0$ (Fidelity: $-0.855$ to $0.197$), characterized by persistent ballistic peaks.
3. **Fractional Langevin Equation:** The Sisyphus cooling models (Fidelity: $-11.417$ to $0.176$), dominated by non-extensive Tsallis statistics and spatially dependent noise.
4. **Deterministic FFP:** The Pomeau-Manneville maps (Fidelity: $-3.901$ to $-1.115$), which exhibit anomalous scaling but fail to converge to a smooth Lévy-stable core due to deterministic artifacts.

**Synthesis of the Minimal Effective Theory:**
These results allow us to explicitly address the core scientific question: which single mechanism constitutes the minimal effective theory for anomalous diffusion? 

While the pure Lévy stable process provides the mathematical ground truth, it is a purely statistical construct devoid of physical mechanism. The CTRW with Lévy jumps offers a highly parsimonious phenomenological description, successfully reproducing the FFP propagator (Fidelity $0.743$, $H=0.683$). However, it is mechanistically tautological, as it generates Lévy flights by explicitly drawing jump lengths from a Lévy distribution. It assumes the existence of the very statistics it seeks to explain.

The most profound candidate for the minimal *physical* effective theory is the **Lévy-Lorentz gas** in the regime $\alpha > 1$ (specifically $\alpha=1.5$). Despite originating from simple, constant-velocity ballistic transport in a random medium—utilizing only Pareto-distributed scatterer spacings without any intrinsic Lévy noise—it achieves an outstanding fidelity score of $0.949$. It perfectly reproduces the Lévy-stable core and tails (KL divergence $0.051$) and exhibits a complete dissolution of ballistic peaks at macroscopic scales.

Therefore, we conclude that while the CTRW serves as the most parsimonious mathematical model for FFP dynamics, the 1D Lévy-Lorentz gas represents the minimal mechanistic effective theory. It successfully bridges purely microscopic geometric disorder to macroscopic fractional diffusion, demonstrating how genuine Lévy-stable statistics and the half-Laplacian operator can emerge organically from velocity-constrained physical systems without requiring non-extensive thermodynamics or tautological jump distributions.
