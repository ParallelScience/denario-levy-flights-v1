The current analysis is technically sound and provides a clear taxonomy of anomalous diffusion mechanisms. However, to elevate this from a descriptive classification to a robust "minimal effective theory," the following critical gaps must be addressed:

1. **The "Minimal Model" Fallacy**: You identify the Lévy-Lorentz gas as the "minimal model" based on AIC/BIC. However, this is a tautology: the Lévy-Lorentz gas is a *direct* simulation of a Lévy-stable process (via Pareto-distributed free paths), whereas the other mechanisms are *emergent* phenomena. You have compared a "generator" to "emergent systems." To strengthen the claim, you must demonstrate if the Lévy-Lorentz gas can *reproduce* the statistics of the other mechanisms (e.g., can you map the Sisyphus cooling parameters to an effective Lorentz gas scatterer distribution?). If not, the "minimal model" is only minimal for a specific class of systems, not a universal effective theory.

2. **Over-reliance on KL Divergence**: Using KL divergence to compare empirical distributions to a Lévy-stable reference is sensitive to the choice of support and the tails. Given that you have established $\ell_{eff}$ (the ultraviolet cutoff), the KL divergence is likely dominated by the "non-Lévy" behavior at small scales. You should instead perform a **tail-weighted KL divergence** or a **Kolmogorov-Smirnov test on the tails only** to isolate the fidelity of the power-law regime from the core dynamics.

3. **Missing Causal Link in Sisyphus Cooling**: You successfully identified that Sisyphus cooling requires a state-dependent $D(x)$. However, you have not tested the *predictive* power of this effective theory. To prove this is a valid "effective theory," you must use the fitted $\theta$ and $D_0$ to simulate a Langevin equation with $D(x)$ and show that it reproduces the original Sisyphus trajectories. Without this "forward" validation, the state-dependent FFP remains a curve-fitting exercise rather than a physical theory.

4. **Actionable Improvement for Future Iterations**:
   - **Bridge the Universality Classes**: Instead of just delineating classes, test if the "Transient" PM maps can be mapped to the "Standard" Lorentz gas by applying a time-dependent transformation. This would unify the classes rather than just listing them.
   - **Sensitivity Analysis of $\ell_{eff}$**: You have calculated $\ell_{eff}$, but you have not tested its stability against trajectory length. If $\ell_{eff}$ scales with the total simulation time, it is an artifact of the finite-size sampling, not a physical property of the mechanism. Perform a scaling check: does $\ell_{eff}$ converge as $N_{steps} \to \infty$?
   - **Simplify the Framework**: The current approach uses DFA, AIC/BIC, FFP mapping, and KL divergence. For the final paper, prioritize the **Characteristic Function analysis** as the primary diagnostic. It is the most direct way to observe the fractional operator's signature and is less prone to the biases of real-space density estimation.

**Summary for next steps**: Focus on the *predictive* validation of the state-dependent FFP for Sisyphus cooling and the *convergence* of $\ell_{eff}$ for the PM maps. If these hold, you have a robust theory; if they fail, the "minimal model" claim must be downgraded to a "phenomenological classification."
