The current analysis is technically competent but suffers from a "model-fitting" bias that obscures the underlying physical mechanisms. While you have successfully identified the CTRW as the most "Lévy-like" model, you have treated the other mechanisms as "failed" approximations rather than distinct physical classes. This is a missed opportunity to define the *limits* of the fractional Fokker-Planck (FFP) effective theory.

**Critical Weaknesses:**
1. **The "Lévy Walk" vs. "Lévy Flight" Conflation:** You correctly identify that the Lévy-Lorentz gas and PM maps exhibit ballistic fronts, yet you penalize them for not being "pure" Lévy flights. This is physically misleading. Lévy flights are mathematical abstractions (infinite velocity); Lévy walks are the physical reality of transport in random media. Your critique should pivot from "which model is the best Lévy flight" to "which physical constraints (e.g., finite velocity) necessitate a transition from the FFP equation to the Generalized Master Equation (GME)."
2. **Over-reliance on KL Divergence:** Using KL divergence against a pure Lévy-stable reference is a blunt instrument. It penalizes the central core of the Sisyphus/Tsallis distributions, which is where the most interesting physics (trapping) occurs. The "high KL divergence" you report for Sisyphus cooling is not a failure of the model; it is a signature of the non-extensive statistics that the FFP equation *cannot* capture.
3. **Redundant Scaling Analysis:** You have performed DFA, MLE tail estimation, and Characteristic Function fitting. These are all essentially measuring the same scaling exponent $H$. You have enough evidence to stop measuring $H$ and start measuring the *crossover* behavior.

**Actionable Recommendations for Future Iterations:**
1. **Shift from "Fit" to "Crossover":** Instead of trying to force all mechanisms into the FFP framework, quantify the *crossover time* $\tau_c$ at which the ballistic/deterministic behavior transitions to the anomalous diffusion regime. For the Lévy-Lorentz gas, define the transition from ballistic ($H=1$) to diffusive ($H=1/\alpha$) scaling. This is more physically insightful than a single $H$ value.
2. **Test the "Minimal Theory" Boundary:** You have identified the CTRW as the minimal model for *flights*. Now, test the minimal model for *walks*. Use the Lévy-Lorentz gas data to see if a simple "Lévy Walk" model (a CTRW with a velocity constraint $v=1$) can bridge the gap between the FFP equation and the observed ballistic fronts. This is a more meaningful "minimal model" than just picking the CTRW.
3. **Drop the BIC/Universality Map:** The BIC ranking is superficial. It tells us that the simplest model is the simplest, which is a tautology. Replace this with a "Mechanism-to-Operator" mapping:
    - If the mechanism is *velocity-constrained* (L-L gas), the effective theory is a *Lévy Walk* (GME).
    - If the mechanism is *friction-dominated* (Sisyphus), the effective theory is a *Fractional Langevin Equation*.
    - If the mechanism is *jump-dominated* (CTRW), the effective theory is the *FFP Equation*.
4. **Focus on the Sisyphus Residuals:** You noted that Sisyphus cooling residuals follow $\alpha$-stable noise. This is your most significant finding. Future work should not be about "how close is this to a Lévy flight," but "can we derive the effective fractional operator directly from the non-linear friction $\gamma(p)$?" This would be a genuine contribution to the field.

Stop trying to prove that everything is a Lévy flight. Start defining the boundaries where the FFP equation breaks down and why.
